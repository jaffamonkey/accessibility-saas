## Where I can help

Useful accessibility work is part evidence, part judgement, and part communication. The aim is to help teams make better decisions quickly, without turning accessibility into theatre.

### 1. Accessibility audits

WCAG-informed reviews combining automated checks, manual testing and practical user-journey review.

- Keyboard and focus behaviour
- Forms, errors and journeys
- Structure, semantics and navigation
- Contrast and visual clarity

### 2. Remediation guidance

Clear reporting for the people who need to act: developers, designers, testers and product owners.

- Prioritised findings
- Plain-English impact notes
- Suggested fixes and examples
- Re-test support after changes

### 3. Systemic analysis

For larger sites, repeated findings can be grouped into patterns so teams can focus on shared causes instead of drowning in repeated rows.

- Repeated issue families
- Shared components and templates
- WCAG concentration
- Fix-once benefit-many views

## Tools help find issues. People judge the experience.

Automated scanners are useful, but they only cover part of accessibility. A useful audit combines tool output with manual checks for keyboard access, focus order, forms, screen reader behaviour, content clarity and real user journeys.

The result should be something a team can act on: what the issue is, why it matters, where it repeats, and what kind of fix is likely to help.

## A straightforward audit flow

1. **Scope** the pages, journeys, risks and assistive technology checks that matter most.
2. **Test** using a blend of manual review, automated checks and systemic analysis where useful.
3. **Prioritise** findings by user impact, repeatability and likely remediation value.
4. **Support** fixes with clear handoff notes, implementation discussion and re-testing.
